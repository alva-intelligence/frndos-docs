# How the Campaign Planner Works

The **Campaign Planner** (Insights → Campaigns → **Planner**) estimates what a
campaign could return *before* you run it. This page explains how it gets to a
number — and, just as importantly, what it does and does not claim. It's written
to be shared with clients.

## In one sentence

The Planner estimates what a campaign could return by taking **how efficiently
each channel has historically turned spend into revenue** — for *your* brand
where we have the data, and for the wider industry where we don't — and applying
that efficiency to the budget you plan to spend, adjusted for seasonality.

## Where the numbers come from

For every channel in your plan, we need one input: its **ROAS** (revenue per
1 currency unit of spend). We source it in a strict order of preference:

1. **Your own logged results first.** Once you close a campaign and log its
   actual revenue, those outcomes become the primary signal for your next plan.
   This is the learning loop — the tool gets more *yours* over time.
2. **Your own platform history.** For paid search and paid social, we read your
   connected ad accounts (Google, Meta, TikTok) and compute the 25th / 50th /
   75th percentile of your historical ROAS.
3. **Industry benchmarks.** Where you don't yet have enough of your own data —
   or for offline channels that don't yet carry attributed revenue (TV, radio,
   OOH, cinema) — we use an industry reference range. *(We may still track your
   spend and reach on those channels; we just don't yet infer a return-on-spend
   for them until you log actual results.)*
4. **A sensible default.** As a last resort, so the tool always shows something,
   we use a conservative typical value.

We always tell you which of these a number came from, on-screen.

:::info Benchmark window
Your own-history ROAS is read from a recent, reliable window of your paid data
(current period), so a prediction reflects how your campaigns perform **now** —
not stale historical periods.
:::

## How a prediction is built

For each channel:

> **planned spend × that channel's ROAS × a seasonality adjustment = predicted revenue**

We sum this across all channels to get total predicted revenue, then derive ROI
and blended ROAS. That's the whole calculation — deliberately transparent, no
black box.

## Three scenarios, not one number

We never show a single false-precision figure. Every plan produces:

- **Conservative** — the lower quartile (25th percentile) of historical efficiency.
- **Typical** — the median (50th percentile).
- **Strong** — the upper quartile (75th percentile).

This range *is* the honest expression of uncertainty: it's the spread of how your
(or the industry's) campaigns have actually performed.

## Confidence levels

The tool grades each channel's prediction by how much of *your* data backs it:

| Badge | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Solid** | 8+ of your own campaigns support this — it's mostly your data. |
| **Limited** | 3–7 campaigns — we blend your data with the industry benchmark and widen the range to reflect the thinner sample. |
| **Benchmark** | Fewer than 3 — this is an industry reference, not your history yet. |

As you run and log more campaigns, channels move up this ladder automatically.

## What this method is — and is not

We believe in being explicit about this, because clients rightly ask.

**It is:** a transparent, data-grounded **planning aid**. Every figure traces
back to a real historical distribution — your own where possible — and the
assumptions are visible, not hidden in a model.

**It is not** (yet) a causal forecast. Specifically:

- **It assumes efficiency stays roughly constant as you scale.** In reality,
  channels saturate — the tenth million spent rarely works as hard as the first.
  Predictions are most reliable near budgets you've actually run before;
  aggressive scale-ups should be read as optimistic.
- **It treats channels independently.** It does not yet model how ATL (TV/radio/
  OOH) can lift the performance of your digital channels, or how upper-funnel
  activity feeds lower-funnel conversion.
- **It describes efficiency, not proven incremental lift.** A high historical
  ROAS tells you spend *correlated* with revenue; it is not a controlled proof
  that the spend *caused* it. Measuring true incrementality requires holdout
  testing (on our roadmap).
- **Offline & organic channels lean on benchmarks.** Until you log actuals for
  them, TV, radio, OOH, cinema, owned and earned use industry references rather
  than your measured results.

## Your data stays yours

All of your own-history calculations run inside **per-workspace isolation
enforced at the database layer** — one client's data is never mixed into another
client's numbers. Within your own workspace, figures are scoped to the brand
you're planning for (or shared deliberately via
[Extended Connections](./core-extended-connections)). Industry benchmarks are
aggregate, third-party reference ranges.

## Where this is heading

Two planned enhancements deepen the rigor:

1. **Validation & calibration.** Using your logged actuals, we back-test the
   Planner's own predictions against what really happened and report the accuracy
   openly — including whether the Conservative–Strong range reliably brackets
   real outcomes.
2. **Marketing-mix modelling.** A statistical model of how each channel's spend
   drives revenue — including diminishing returns and cross-channel halo effects —
   which will let us answer questions like *"is our ATL spend actually moving the
   revenue needle?"* with measured elasticities rather than assumptions.
